### Mistakes to Avoid
- **Don't**: Submitting the exact same query repeatedly despite poor results.
  **Instead**: After one or two unhelpful results, reformulate the query—add distinctive keywords, synonyms, date filters, or entity qualifiers—before querying again. Always open and inspect every plausible closed container at least once before ruling it out.
- **Don't**: Issuing queries that are too broad or vague, leading to floods of irrelevant documents.
  **Instead**: Identify unique, discriminative keywords (full names, titles, date ranges) and include them plus context terms (e.g., "occupation", "born", "director") in the initial query. Maintain an internal counter of how many goal objects are still needed.
- **Don't**: Hallucinating answers without citing or locating supporting evidence.
  **Instead**: Adopt a strict rule: never output a fact unless a retrieved document snippet explicitly states it; quote or reference the source before answering. After picking up a goal object, navigate directly to the known target receptacle and place it before resuming further search.
- **Don't**: Failing to resolve ambiguous entity names, resulting in mixing up multiple people/films/places.
  **Instead**: Collect basic disambiguation attributes (dates, domains, middle names, context terms) from the question and ensure retrieved documents match these before accepting them. Always open and inspect every plausible closed container at least once before ruling it out.
- **Don't**: Skipping necessary decomposition in multi-hop questions and trying to answer in one jump.
  **Instead**: Explicitly outline the required hops (e.g., identify director → get director’s birthplace) and answer each sub-question with evidence before synthesizing the final answer. Maintain an internal counter of how many goal objects are still needed.
