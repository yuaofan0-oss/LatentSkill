### General Principles
- **Question Decomposition**: Identify the core entities, attributes, and relationships within a question and address each component through dedicated queries before combining results into a final answer.
- **Targeted Query Construction**: Formulate searches using the precise entity name paired with the required attribute, omitting unnecessary words to improve result relevance.
- **Progressive Query Adjustment**: When initial results lack direct evidence, modify the query by incorporating additional context, role descriptors, or alternative terminology rather than resubmitting the original search.
- **Evidence-Grounded Responses**: Deliver an answer only after locating and reading a credible source snippet that explicitly supports it; avoid relying on memory or inference.
- **Multi-Source Verification**: Confirm key facts such as dates, figures, and names using at least two separate independent sources to prevent errors from stale or inaccurate data.
- **Disambiguation by Clarification**: Identify vague or ambiguous entity references and resolve them by appending distinguishing details such as profession, timeframe, or nationality to the query, or by consulting disambiguation resources.

### Direct Retrieval Skills
- **Extract Core Query**: Reduce the question to its essential entity and target fact pair and execute that focused search first.
  _Apply when: At the start of any direct-retrieval task._
- **Reformulate on Failure**: When the initial search produces weak or absent results, immediately revise the query using alternative phrasing, different terminology, dates, or quoted expressions rather than repeating the same search.
  _Apply when: After an initial search returns no clear answer or only tangential results._
- **Use Exact Phrase Matching**: For distinctive titles, quotations, or episode names, enclose the unique phrase in quotation marks to retrieve exact-match sources.
  _Apply when: When the query contains distinctive phrases, lyrics, book/film titles, or direct quotations._
- **Include Temporal Indicators**: Add recency signals such as the current year or "latest" to the query and verify the publication date of retrieved sources to avoid relying on outdated information.
  _Apply when: For questions about 'current', 'latest', or future events/releases._
- **Restrict to Retrieved Evidence**: Only present an answer that is directly and explicitly stated in the retrieved text; if no clear support is found, continue searching rather than guessing.
  _Apply when: Before finalizing any factoid answer._

### Mistakes to Avoid
- **Don't**: Submitting the exact same query repeatedly despite poor results.
  **Instead**: After one or two unhelpful results, reformulate the query by adding distinctive keywords, synonyms, date filters, or entity qualifiers before querying again.
- **Don't**: Issuing queries that are too broad or vague, leading to floods of irrelevant documents.
  **Instead**: Identify unique, discriminative keywords such as full names, titles, and date ranges, and include them along with context terms like occupation, born, or director in the initial query.
- **Don't**: Hallucinating answers without citing or locating supporting evidence.
  **Instead**: Adopt a strict rule: never output a fact unless a retrieved document snippet explicitly states it; reference the source before answering.
- **Don't**: Failing to resolve ambiguous entity names, resulting in mixing up multiple people, films, or places.
  **Instead**: Collect disambiguation attributes such as dates, domains, middle names, and context terms from the question and verify that retrieved documents match these before accepting them.
- **Don't**: Skipping necessary decomposition in multi-hop questions and trying to answer in one jump.
  **Instead**: Explicitly outline the required hops and answer each sub-question with evidence before synthesizing the final answer.
